# Clinician Language Style Guide v1

## Purpose
This style guide standardises the clinical register for the HealthIQ Clinician Summary Report (v1). It prevents tone drift and enforces safe, non-prescriptive language.

This guide applies to all clinician report text fields:
- `header.disclaimer_top`
- `sections.page1.*` (summary block)
- `sections.hypotheses.*` (titles, summaries, ranking rationale, evidence items)
- `sections.confirmatory_tests.*` (display text + rationales)
- `data_quality.*` (caveats, quality notes)

The KB-S31 denylist applies on top of this guide.

---

## Required register
Write as a clinical investigation summary, not patient-facing education.
- concise
- neutral
- evidence-led
- explicitly bounded by available data

Avoid motivational tone, reassurance tone, or lifestyle coaching tone.

---

## Approved verbs and phrasing

### Approved verbs (use these)
- suggests
- is consistent with
- may indicate
- may be associated with
- cannot exclude
- warrants consideration of
- consider discussing with your clinician
- consider including in the next panel
- consider repeating in X weeks
- limited by missing data

### Approved uncertainty framing
- “On this panel alone…”
- “Given available markers…”
- “This differential is limited by…”
- “A higher-confidence distinction would require…”

### Approved referral framing
- “Consider discussing this pattern with your clinician.”
- “Clinical review may be appropriate, particularly if persistent or symptomatic.”
- “This report is a structured discussion aid.”

---

## Prohibited tone markers (patient-facing language)
Do not use:
- “don’t worry”
- “good news”
- “great”
- “you should feel”
- “take control”
- “your body is telling you”
- “biohack”
- “longevity optimisation” (clinician report only)

---

## Prohibited clinical certainty (diagnosis language)
Do not use:
- confirms
- diagnoses
- rules out
- definitive
- “you have…”
- “you are…”
- “this means you have…”

Use “suggests / consistent with / cannot exclude”.

---

## Prohibited prescriptive language (ordering / prescribing)
Do not use:
- “order this test”
- “you need this test”
- “start / stop / increase / decrease” (medications)
- dosing language
- treatment claims (including “reverse” language)

Use:
- “consider discussing X testing”
- “consider including X in the next panel”

---

## Evidence statement rules
Evidence summaries must describe study findings only:
- population context (if known)
- direction of association
- effect size (if known)
- avoid causal overclaims unless consensus guideline states it

No new clinical claims beyond what is already in governed knowledge assets.

---

## Formatting rules
- short sentences
- no rhetorical questions
- no emotive adjectives
- no emojis
- no marketing language
- avoid “we” voice; write in neutral third-person clinical register

---

## Examples

### Bad (patient-facing)
“Your homocysteine is high. You should improve your diet and take supplements.”

### Good (clinician-facing)
“Homocysteine is elevated. This pattern may be consistent with one-carbon metabolism stress; differential assessment is limited by missing confirmatory markers.”
