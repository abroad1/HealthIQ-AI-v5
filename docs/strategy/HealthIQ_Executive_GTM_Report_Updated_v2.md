# HealthIQ AI — Executive Go-To-Market Plan (Updated v2)

## Purpose

Define the most commercially credible route from current product maturity to enterprise revenue, dataset moat, and strategic-buyer relevance.

This update preserves the core strategic direction of the earlier GTM paper while hard-baking in one critical correction:

**buyers do not buy HealthIQ because it is intelligent; they buy it because they are better off afterwards.**

It also sharpens one important positioning point:

**HealthIQ is not a testing company, lab, or blood collection business. It is a post-test metabolic intelligence layer that sits on top of already-tested blood panels.**

That means the GTM plan must be organised around buyer outcomes, not product mechanisms, and must describe HealthIQ as an interpretation and stratification layer rather than anything that implies lab ownership or test fulfilment.

---

## 1. Executive summary

- HealthIQ should not enter the market as another AI blood-report app. It should position as **scalable post-test metabolic interpretation infrastructure**: deterministic, traceable, low-cost, and capable of processing very large cohorts for labs, insurers, employers, and public-sector prevention programmes.
- The strongest commercial edge is not just deeper analysis. It is **deeper analysis at industrial scale**: deterministic processing, lower unit cost, faster batch throughput, reproducible outputs, and clearer audit trails than LLM-first competitors.
- The best near-term wedge is not creating a new market from scratch. It is entering markets where blood panels are already being collected, uploaded, or screened and improving interpretation, stratification, and re-test conversion.
- Recommended market-entry sequence remains: **(1) labs / blood panel companies, (2) insurers and population-health partners, (3) large workforce and public-sector screening programmes.**
- The GTM plan must now be run on an **outcome-first discipline**: what is better for the buyer after HealthIQ is in place, what metric changes, and why that matters economically.

---

## 2. Core thesis

The market does not want “AI blood interpretation” in the abstract.

It wants one of four outcomes:

- more revenue
- lower cost
- less risk
- faster / better decisions

HealthIQ therefore should not sell “analysis” as the product. It should sell the **post-adoption state**:

- labs make more money from their existing customer base
- insurers identify future cost-risk earlier and more defensibly
- workforce screening providers prioritise follow-up more effectively
- clinicians review complex panels faster with clearer synthesis

This is possible because HealthIQ is not burdened by testing operations. It is an **asset-light interpretation layer** that can sit across existing panel sources rather than competing in test fulfilment.

That is the shift from product-led GTM to outcome-led GTM.

## 3. Positioning statement

**HealthIQ is a scalable post-test metabolic interpretation layer that sits on top of existing blood testing, screening, and prevention workflows and makes those workflows more commercially and clinically valuable afterwards.**

### Do not lead with

- AI blood-test explainer
- testing company language
- dashboard product
- chat-style insights
- marker-by-marker commentary
- “more intelligent” analysis

### Lead with

- post-test metabolic intelligence
- revenue unlock from existing screened populations
- explainable metabolic stratification
- lower-cost population-scale interpretation
- reproducible and traceable cohort analysis
- panel-level synthesis and follow-up prioritisation
- lab-agnostic interpretation infrastructure

The key refinement is that HealthIQ should be described as sitting **after the test, not inside the test**.

---

## 4. Why this positioning is strategically stronger

Because HealthIQ does not take blood and does not run a lab, the company avoids the main traps of the testing market:

- no lab infrastructure burden
- no collection logistics
- no fulfilment race to the bottom
- no dependence on one testing supply chain
- no need to win on price-per-panel economics

Instead, HealthIQ can position as:

- asset-light
- lab-agnostic
- easy to layer onto existing panel sources
- suitable for both retail-upload and enterprise-upload workflows
- able to work across multiple existing testing ecosystems

This makes the company easier to partner with and less exposed to test commoditisation.

## 6. Market-entry sequence

The sequencing remains strategically sound:

1. labs / blood panel companies
2. insurers / population-health partners
3. large workforce / public-sector screening

The rationale is unchanged:

- labs are the fastest route to volume and realistic panels
- insurers are the clearest medium-term economic buyer
- workforce and public-sector screening are potentially the largest contracts but require more proof and longer cycles

The refinement is that HealthIQ should now be pitched more explicitly as a **layer on top of their existing testing infrastructure**, not as a competing testing provider.

---

## 7. Buyer outcome map

### 7.1 Labs / blood panel companies

#### Desired future
- higher revenue per customer
- more re-tests
- more upsell / add-on panels
- stronger customer retention
- more value from the same existing customer base

#### Costly problem today
Many labs and blood panel companies stop at basic marker commentary. “Your LDL is high” is a weak commercial trigger. It does not create much urgency, differentiation, or re-engagement value.

#### How HealthIQ changes the outcome
HealthIQ can sit on top of existing panels and surface:
- deeper panel-level synthesis
- likely metabolic patterns
- stronger reasons to re-test
- premium interpretation products
- cohort-level commercial opportunities across the installed base

#### What the buyer is better off with afterwards
- dormant customers can be reactivated
- existing customers can be converted into repeat customers
- interpretation becomes a revenue engine rather than a cost centre
- the lab gains access to a differentiated premium layer without becoming an AI company itself

#### Additional strategic benefit
Because HealthIQ is not a competing lab, it is easier to position as an **incremental commercial layer** rather than an operational replacement.

### 7.2 Insurers / population-health partners

#### Desired future
- fewer avoidable claims
- earlier identification of future cost-risk
- more targeted intervention spending
- a more defensible prevention story internally

#### Costly problem today
Insurers are incentivised to identify future cost early, but much of the market is still either:
- too shallow
- too generic
- too black-box
- or not sufficiently explainable for enterprise use

#### How HealthIQ changes the outcome
HealthIQ can provide:
- explainable metabolic stratification
- earlier visibility into emerging metabolic deterioration
- prioritisation of members for follow-up or intervention
- more traceable reasoning than a generic AI explainer

#### What the buyer is better off with afterwards
- intervention budgets can be directed more intelligently
- risk segmentation becomes more explainable
- prevention efforts become more measurable
- black-box discomfort is reduced

#### Additional strategic benefit
Because HealthIQ is not tied to a single test supply chain, it can potentially sit across multiple existing panel sources in the insurer’s ecosystem.

### 7.3 Large workforce / public-sector screening

#### Desired future
- fewer people drifting into expensive sickness
- earlier follow-up on silent metabolic deterioration
- lower cost of repeat screening
- better prioritisation of scarce clinical or occupational-health resources

#### Costly problem today
Large screening programmes often stop at:
- data collection
- dashboarding
- basic commentary
- generic health advice

That is weak if the goal is prevention economics or operational readiness.

#### How HealthIQ changes the outcome
HealthIQ can provide:
- low-cost repeatable cohort interpretation
- prioritisation of who most needs follow-up
- pattern-level metabolic stratification
- consistent processing across very large populations

#### What the buyer is better off with afterwards
- screening becomes more actionable
- follow-up becomes more targeted
- large cohorts can be processed economically
- prevention programmes can move from visibility to prioritisation

#### Additional strategic benefit
HealthIQ can be inserted without requiring the buyer to replace existing phlebotomy, lab, or test procurement arrangements.

## 8. Existing market vs new market

HealthIQ does not need to invent population-scale screening demand from scratch.

The more credible path is to enter markets where:
- blood panels are already being collected or uploaded
- cohorts are already being screened
- prevention workflows already exist
- the interpretation layer is still weak

The company is not trying to persuade the market that testing exists. It is trying to persuade the market that **already-tested panels become more valuable afterwards when HealthIQ sits on top of them**.

That is a cleaner and stronger story than anything that implies HealthIQ is part of the lab commodity layer.

---

## 9. Product-surface implications for GTM

The product surface must support this positioning.

If the output surface does not visibly show:
- what matters most
- what is plausible vs uncertain
- what should happen next
- why this is more than a dashboard

then the GTM story weakens.

So the frontend should reinforce the market position by making visible:
- prioritisation
- synthesis
- uncertainty handling
- follow-up relevance

not just graphs and charts.

The product must feel like a **post-test decision-support and stratification layer**, not a prettier lab portal.

---

## 10. Commercial KPIs

The KPI set remains:

- average processing time per report
- maximum reports processed per hour in batch
- cost per report and gross margin at enterprise volume
- re-test / add-on conversion uplift for lab partners
- repeat testing rate and longitudinal retention
- consistency and reproducibility of outputs

Add one more discipline:

For each target segment, define the **after-metric**:
- what is measurably better afterwards?

Examples:
- lab: re-test revenue per 1,000 customers
- insurer: proportion of members correctly prioritised for intervention
- workforce screening: follow-up yield per 1,000 screened individuals
- clinician partner: minutes saved per complex panel review

## 11. Strategic risks

| Risk | Why it matters | Required response |
|---|---|---|
| Market accepts shallow AI explanation as sufficient | Better architecture alone will not win | Make business value visible quickly |
| Competitors win on polish and distribution | HealthIQ risks being technically stronger but commercially quieter | Invest in partner packaging and proof |
| Public-sector cycles are slow | Revenue could lag | Use faster wedges first |
| Data moat grows too slowly | Exit story weakens | Use partners that already own panel volume |
| Product surface becomes too technical | Buyer sees engine internals, not buyer value | Keep the surface outcome-led and translated |
| Product surface becomes too shallow | Buyer sees another dashboard | Make synthesis, prioritisation, and follow-up relevance visible |
| Messaging implies testing-provider status | HealthIQ gets mentally pulled into the commodity lab market | Keep all messaging anchored on post-test interpretation and stratification |
