# Proposal: Use of “Phenotype” as a Core HealthIQ Market Term

## Purpose

This note proposes that HealthIQ adopt **phenotype** as a core product and market term, used alongside plain-English descriptors, rather than relying only on generic system labels such as heart, liver, renal, or blood sugar.

The goal is not to sound more academic for its own sake.  
The goal is to create a recognisable, ownable market language for the HealthIQ interpretation model.

---

## Why this matters

If we describe our product only in generic terms such as:

- cardiovascular risk
- liver markers
- kidney function
- blood sugar

then we sound like every other blood-test explainer in the market.

There is little brand capture in those phrases because they are generic category language.

By contrast, **phenotype** gives us a more distinctive and medically serious frame for how HealthIQ interprets a blood panel:

- not isolated markers
- not generic organ summaries
- but identifiable **metabolic phenotypes** and phenotype patterns

This better reflects our real USP.

---

## Strategic proposal

HealthIQ should position itself as the company that makes **phenotype-based blood interpretation** understandable and commercially useful.

Over time, we want the market association to become:

**phenotype-based blood interpretation = HealthIQ**

This gives us a better chance of owning a recognisable interpretation category rather than competing only as another blood panel explainer.

---

## Why “phenotype” works

### 1. It reflects the real product
HealthIQ does not merely explain single biomarkers.  
It identifies broader biological patterns across multiple markers.

Phenotype is a better fit for that than isolated system labels.

### 2. It creates category differentiation
Most competitors talk about:
- biomarkers
- trends
- high / low values
- risk
- optimisation

Phenotype gives us a more distinctive interpretive lens.

### 3. It adds medical gravitas
Phenotype is a real medical / scientific term, especially in metabolic and cardiometabolic contexts.

Used well, it helps position HealthIQ as a serious medical-grade interpretation platform rather than a consumer wellness dashboard.

### 4. It is more ownable than generic system language
We cannot own “heart health” or “kidney function.”
We have a much better chance of becoming strongly associated with phenotype-led interpretation.

---

## B2C positioning

For retail users, we should **not** use phenotype on its own.

We should pair it with plain-English explanation.

Examples:

- **Insulin Resistance Phenotype**  
  A pattern suggesting early impaired sugar and lipid handling

- **Liver Stress Phenotype**  
  A pattern suggesting metabolic strain in the liver

- **Inflammatory Phenotype**  
  A pattern suggesting persistent low-grade inflammation

This gives us:
- differentiation
- education
- clarity
- seriousness without confusion

The retail objective is:
- teach the term gradually
- make it understandable
- build familiarity over time

---

## B2B positioning

For clinicians, labs, insurers, and enterprise buyers, phenotype is stronger.

In B2B settings, phenotype helps us position HealthIQ as:

- a phenotype interpretation layer
- a phenotype stratification engine
- a phenotype-based metabolic intelligence platform

This is more distinctive than calling ourselves a blood report company.

It also better supports future enterprise use cases such as:

- phenotype-level stratification
- population screening
- cohort prioritisation
- longitudinal phenotype movement
- intervention response by phenotype

For B2B buyers, phenotype is not just branding language.  
It supports the economic story of HealthIQ as infrastructure for segmentation and decision support.

---

## Recommendation on terminology

We should adopt a **dual-language approach**:

### External / user-facing
Use:
- phenotype + plain-English descriptor
- phenotype + explanation

### Internal / technical / enterprise-facing
Use phenotype more directly and systematically as part of our core model, reporting, segmentation, and market language.

This lets us:
- stay clear for retail users
- stay distinctive in the market
- stay credible with serious buyers

---

## Technical note for the product and development teams

This terminology decision is not just cosmetic.

If we commit to phenotype as a core market concept, then product and technical work should remain consistent with that direction.

That means:

- phenotype labels should be stable and governed
- phenotype logic should map cleanly to underlying signal and interpretation architecture
- phenotype naming should be translated carefully for frontend use
- phenotype language should not drift into inconsistent synonyms across product surfaces
- longitudinal product design should consider future concepts such as phenotype persistence, phenotype shift, and phenotype improvement / deterioration

In short:

**if phenotype becomes a market term, it must also be a disciplined product term.**

---

## Proposed decision

Approve phenotype as a core HealthIQ interpretation and positioning term, used with plain-English descriptors for retail users and used more directly in clinician, enterprise, and technical contexts.

This would position HealthIQ not just as a company that explains blood tests, but as the company defining **phenotype-based metabolic interpretation**.
