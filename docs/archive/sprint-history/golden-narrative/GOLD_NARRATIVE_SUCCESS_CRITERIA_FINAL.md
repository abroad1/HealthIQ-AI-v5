# Gold Narrative Success Criteria v5

## Purpose

This document defines the target standard for a gold-standard HealthIQ narrative.

It is not a frontend specification.
It is not a backend contract.
It is not a draft of the final report itself.

It is the locked reference note for what the narrative must achieve if HealthIQ is to deliver a world-class, consultation-grade blood interpretation experience.

The purpose of this note is to ensure that narrative design is judged against the right benchmark.

HealthIQ is not trying to produce a slightly better blood report.
HealthIQ is aiming to produce a consultation-grade interpretation experience that feels comparable to the depth, coherence, personalisation, and explanatory power of an exceptional premium specialist consultation.

If the narrative feels like automated commentary, generic wellness writing, or a polished lab summary, it has failed.

---

## 1. Core benchmark

The benchmark is not ordinary blood-report software.
The benchmark is premium human interpretation.

The gold narrative should feel like the user has received the kind of deep, integrated, biologically literate interpretation they might expect from an exceptional high-end specialist consultation.

That does not mean imitating clinical pomp or sounding theatrical.
It means the narrative must feel:

- deeply informed
- coherent across systems
- highly personal
- biologically serious
- educational in a memorable way
- evidence-anchored
- capable of prioritising what matters most

The user should feel interpreted, not processed.

---

## 2. End-state user impression

By the end of the narrative, the user should feel:

- my body has been read as one connected biological system, not as a list of markers
- the report can see what is strong, not only what is wrong
- the main interpretation feels earned and explained
- I can see how one system may be affecting another
- the report noticed where my lifestyle and my bloods align, conflict, or complicate each other
- my questionnaire responses changed the interpretation in a meaningful way
- uncertainty has been handled honestly
- I have learned something memorable about my own biology
- I understand what matters most next, not just what is interesting
- this felt closer to a premium specialist consultation than to a lab summary

---

## 3. Core success criteria

A gold-standard HealthIQ narrative must satisfy all of the following.

### 3.1 Whole-system reading
The body must be interpreted as an interconnected system.
Single markers may matter, but they must gain meaning through relationship, pattern, and body-wide context.

### 3.2 Balanced interpretation
The narrative must identify resilience, stability, and protective features as well as strain, burden, and concern.
A report that only scans for problems is not world class.

### 3.3 Earned lead interpretation
The leading line of interpretation must feel justified.
The narrative must make clear why this pattern leads, what supports it, what complicates it, and what remains uncertain.

### 3.4 Personal relevance
The report must feel like it is reading this panel and this person.
It must not read like generic biomarker education pasted onto lab results.

### 3.5 Lifestyle-context integration
The narrative must interpret biomarkers in the context of declared lifestyle, behaviours, exposures, and relevant health context, not as if the blood panel exists in a vacuum.

It should identify when reported behaviour:

- helps explain the observed pattern
- strengthens the significance of a result
- softens or reframes an apparent abnormality
- conflicts with what the blood panel appears to show
- suggests expected adaptation versus unresolved dysfunction

The user should feel that their questionnaire responses materially shaped the interpretation.

### 3.6 Honest uncertainty
If the data is suggestive rather than conclusive, the narrative must say so.
If competing interpretations remain plausible, the narrative must say so.
If missing data would materially sharpen the picture, the narrative must say so.

### 3.7 Costly stability and trade-offs
The narrative must recognise that normal does not always mean effortless.
Some apparently stable states may reflect active compensation, metabolic cost, or fragile equilibrium rather than robust ease.

Where supported, the report should identify when stability appears to be maintained through trade-offs between systems.

### 3.8 Protective and compensatory responses
An abnormal result should not automatically be framed as damage or failure.
In context, it may represent buffering, defence, adaptation, or compensation.

Where supported, the narrative should identify protective or compensatory responses that appear to be helping maintain function.

### 3.9 Directional cross-system logic
The prose must not merely state that multiple things are present at once.
It should show directional biological relationships where justified: one system burdening, restraining, compensating for, responding to, or interacting with another.

### 3.10 Prioritised resolution
The narrative must not end with explanation alone.
Where the evidence is strong enough, it should identify the highest-leverage next move: the single most important follow-up priority, clarification, discussion point, test, monitoring step, or behavioural lever that most changes the picture.

This is not a requirement to reduce everything to one lifestyle action.
It is a requirement to leave the user with a clear hierarchy, not a diffuse cloud of advice.

### 3.11 Conditional time awareness
Where prior results exist, the narrative should interpret change over time, not just the current snapshot.
Where no longitudinal data exists, the report must not pretend to know trajectory with confidence.

---

## 4. Mandatory narrative qualities

The gold narrative must be:

- systems-level
- biologically vivid
- medically serious
- personalised
- non-alarmist
- deterministic-feeling
- elegant and readable
- evidence-anchored
- transparent about uncertainty
- context-aware
- capable of showing mechanistic or directional logic without overclaiming
- capable of prioritisation rather than equal-weight commentary

---

## 5. Core narrative jobs

A gold-standard HealthIQ narrative must perform the following jobs.

### 5.1 Orient the user
Open by showing the body as a whole, not by dropping the user into isolated evidence.

### 5.2 Reassure where justified
Identify what appears stable, resilient, or well-regulated, supported by evidence.

### 5.3 Name the lead pattern
State the main line of interpretation clearly and explain why it matters.

### 5.4 Explain how the body story works
Show how systems connect, how one finding changes the meaning of another, and how body-wide context alters significance.

### 5.5 Integrate lifestyle context
Connect the user’s declared behaviours, exposures, habits, or relevant context to the observed biological patterns.

The narrative should show when lifestyle context:

- explains a result
- complicates a result
- makes a result more surprising
- suggests adaptation
- suggests that something is still unresolved despite apparently favourable behaviour

### 5.6 Surface trade-offs and compensation
Identify where one system appears to be carrying load for another, where stability may be metabolically costly, and where a result may reflect protection or buffering rather than simple dysfunction.

### 5.7 Make evidence feel meaningful
Use biomarkers as evidence, but do not let the report collapse into marker-by-marker sludge.
Evidence should support the story, not bury it.

### 5.8 Make uncertainty legible
Explain what remains ambiguous, what alternative explanations exist, and what missing information would most improve confidence.

### 5.9 Identify what matters most next
Leave the user with a clear sense of the highest-leverage next move, not merely a bundle of loosely related suggestions.

That next move may be:

- a confirmatory test
- a clinician discussion
- a medication or supplement review
- repeat testing
- a behavioural change
- a missing-data clarification

The narrative should show which next move most changes the picture and why.

---

## 6. What the narrative must avoid

Do not produce:

- shallow wellness copy
- generic health-blog language
- repetitive lab-report phrasing
- empty educational filler
- templated “your result is high/low” commentary
- rigid dependence on current UI ordering if that harms the story
- vague feel-good prose that is not biologically anchored
- decorative use of questionnaire data without true interpretive impact
- false certainty about lifestyle causation when the evidence is weak
- static-snapshot thinking that ignores trajectory when longitudinal data exists
- endings that dissolve into equally weighted, generic, or diffuse advice

Do not make up precise facts about the user that are not supported.
Do not become alarmist.
Do not sacrifice medical seriousness for drama.

---

## 7. Failure modes

The narrative has failed if any of the following are true:

- it feels like software-generated commentary rather than consultation-grade interpretation
- it reads as a list of markers rather than a connected body story
- it only identifies problems and does not notice resilience or protection
- it states a lead interpretation without making it feel earned
- it ignores the user’s declared lifestyle or treats it as decorative background
- it forces stronger lifestyle conclusions than the data supports
- it treats all “normal” markers as if they automatically represent robust health
- it cannot recognise compensatory or protective responses when they are central to the story
- it describes co-existing findings without showing directional biological relationships
- it treats the body as a static snapshot with no temporal logic where prior data exists
- it ends with generic or equally weighted next steps rather than a clear hierarchy of what matters most next
- it sounds polished but not biologically insightful

---

## 8. Gold-standard test

A narrative meets the standard only if a thoughtful user could plausibly finish it and say:

- this felt like someone truly understood my body as a connected system
- this felt personalised to my data and to what I told the system about my life
- this explained not just what looked off, but what appeared stable and why
- this helped me see compensation, trade-offs, or protective responses I would never have noticed on my own
- this was intellectually honest about uncertainty
- this told me what matters most next, not just everything that could be said
- this felt closer to an elite private interpretation than to software output

If the user instead feels they received a polished automated summary, the standard has not been met.

---

## 9. Design implication

This standard implies that HealthIQ must not treat narrative as cosmetic prose layered on top of biomarker flags.

To meet this benchmark consistently, the system must be able to support:

- whole-body interpretation
- lifestyle-aware interpretation
- compensation-aware interpretation
- strong uncertainty handling
- prioritised next-move logic
- consultation-grade coherence

That is the standard the narrative layer must aspire to meet.
