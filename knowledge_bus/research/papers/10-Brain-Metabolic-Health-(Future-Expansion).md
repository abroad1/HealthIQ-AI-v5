# 1. Executive Summary

The **Brain Metabolic Health** signal evaluates the influence of metabolic biomarkers on long-term brain resilience and dementia risk.  It is grounded in the concept of “type‐3 diabetes,” where systemic insulin resistance, dysglycemia, dyslipidemia and inflammation adversely affect neuronal function and neurovascular health【26†L302-L308】【45†L321-L325】.  Clinically, this signal asks whether an individual’s metabolic profile (insulin resistance, chronic inflammation, lipid patterns, blood pressure, etc.) suggests higher risk of future cognitive decline or reduced brain health.  In practice, we recommend using the **triglyceride–glucose (TyG) index** – a composite of fasting triglycerides and glucose – as the primary metric, since it integrates lipid and glycemic information and has evidence linking it to cognitive outcomes【58†L13-L21】【86†L317-L321】.  However, the evidence base is observational: multiple cohort studies show insulin resistance and hyperglycemia in midlife predict faster cognitive decline【45†L321-L325】【51†L338-L347】, and metabolic syndrome components (obesity, high cholesterol, hypertension) at midlife additively increase dementia risk【79†L317-L326】.  Although the evidence quality is moderate (prospective cohorts and a few trials), no specific blood-test thresholds for cognitive risk have been firmly validated. For example, intensive glucose lowering in diabetics did **not** improve cognitive scores【90†L7-L10】.  In sum, we tentatively endorse *TyG index* (with established conversion factors for platform units) as the signal’s metric, but stress that **threshold evidence is currently inconclusive**, so these risk tiers must be interpreted with caution.

# 2. Biological Question

> **"Based on my blood test results, are my metabolic markers signalling an increased risk of brain dysfunction or cognitive decline in the future?"**

This signal specifically asks how features of metabolic syndrome (insulin resistance, dysglycemia, dyslipidemia, chronic inflammation, vascular strain) relate to long-term brain health and resilience against dementia.

# 3. Clinical Rationale

Metabolic dysfunction (especially insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes) is a well‐established risk factor for cognitive impairment and dementia【26†L293-L302】【45†L321-L325】.  Chronically elevated glucose and insulin impair neuronal insulin signalling and promote neuroinflammation and amyloid-β accumulation【26†L302-L308】.  Dyslipidemia also affects brain health: midlife obesity, high cholesterol and high blood pressure each roughly double later dementia risk【79†L317-L326】.  Conversely, chronic inflammation (e.g. elevated CRP/IL-6) reflects systemic inflammation that can drive microvascular damage and exacerbate neurodegeneration.  By integrating these markers, the signal targets early/intermediate stages of metabolic brain injury – well before dementia onset – when interventions (glucose control, diet/exercise, risk factor management) are more likely to preserve cognition.  Early detection matters because many at-risk individuals do not yet have overt diabetes or cardiovascular disease, yet may already harbor brain changes; identifying them enables risk reduction and monitoring.  Importantly, however, intensive lowering of glucose alone has *not* conclusively shown cognitive benefits【90†L7-L10】, highlighting that this signal is prognostic rather than prescriptive on its own.

# 5. Required Biomarkers

- **glucose** — Fasting plasma glucose; central to glycemic status and insulin resistance signal. **Why**: Elevated glucose (even subdiabetic) is implicated in cognitive decline【26†L302-L308】【69†L336-L344】. **Fasting required?** Yes (to standardize insulin resistance measures). **SSOT unit:** `glucose` in **mmol/L**.

- **triglycerides** — Fasting triglycerides; reflects lipid metabolism. **Why**: Used in the TyG index (see below), linking dyslipidemia to insulin resistance and brain risk【58†L13-L21】. **Fasting required?** Yes (postprandial TG are variable). **SSOT unit:** `triglycerides` in **mmol/L**.

- **[Optional] hdl_cholesterol** — High-density lipoprotein. **Why**: HDL supports cholesterol transport in the brain and may inversely relate to cognitive decline risk (though evidence is mixed). **Fasting?** Preferred for consistency (lipid panel). **Unit:** `hdl_cholesterol` in **mmol/L**.

- **[Optional] crp** — C-reactive protein. **Why**: Marker of systemic inflammation; can indicate chronic inflammatory burden affecting the brain. **Fasting?** No. **Unit:** `crp` in **mg/L**.

- **[Optional] systolic_bp** — Systolic blood pressure (from lifestyle registry). **Why**: Midlife hypertension is a modifiable vascular risk for cognitive impairment. **Fasting?** N/A. **Unit:** `systolic_bp` in **mmHg**.

*(Additional anthropometric/lifestyle inputs like BMI, waist-to-height ratio, smoking, etc., can be considered as complementary risk factors but are not required biomarkers for this metabolic signal.)*

# 6. Derived Metrics and Formulas

- **tyg_index** — **Formula:** `tyg_index = ln((triglycerides_mg_dl * glucose_mg_dl) / 2)`.  Inputs: triglycerides and glucose must be in mg/dL. For platform units: multiply triglycerides (mmol/L) by 88.6 and glucose (mmol/L) by 18 to convert to mg/dL. **Evidence anchor:** TyG is a validated surrogate of insulin resistance linked to metabolic outcomes【58†L13-L21】【86†L317-L321】. **Existing in platform?** yes (already implemented).
  
- **(Optional) non_hdl_cholesterol** — (platform-computed). **Formula:** `non_hdl_cholesterol = total_cholesterol - hdl_cholesterol`.  **Unit:** mmol/L. **Evidence anchor:** Broad cardiovascular risk; used to contextualize dyslipidemia (though no direct brain-specific threshold). **Existing in platform?** yes.
  
- **(Optional) ldl_hdl_ratio** — (platform). **Formula:** `ldl_hdl_ratio = ldl_cholesterol / hdl_cholesterol`. **Unitless.** **Evidence anchor:** Simple dyslipidemia ratio; higher ratios indicate atherogenic profile (brain health uses are indirect). **Existing in platform?** yes.

*(No new derived metrics beyond TyG are strictly required for the core signal, but the platform’s existing non-HDL and LDL/HDL can complement interpretation.)*

# 8. Override Rules and Guardrails

- **Condition:** Diagnosed type 2 diabetes mellitus (HbA1c ≥ 6.5% or on anti-diabetic medication)  
  **Resulting state:** *at_risk* regardless of TyG index.  
  **Evidence:** Established that diabetes substantially elevates dementia risk (diabetics have ~2–2.5× higher risk of cognitive decline/dementia)【26†L293-L302】【62†L84-L91】, so any diabetic individual should be flagged at-risk by this metabolic-brain signal.

*(Other clinical diagnoses of cognitive impairment (e.g. stroke, MCI) are separate considerations but outside this metabolic signal. Likewise, very high single markers (e.g. acute CRP elevation >10 mg/L indicating infection) should prompt exclusion or re-testing rather than altering signal state.)*

# 9. Clinical Limitations and Excluded Populations

- **Populations to exclude or interpret with caution:**  Most evidence comes from middle-aged or older adults (40–80 yrs) of European or Asian descent; applicability to younger adults (<40), pediatric, or diverse ethnic groups is uncertain.  Studies often excluded those with known stroke, dementia, or insulin-treated diabetes.  Women were underrepresented in some cohorts (e.g. ARIC, HUNT), while others included only women (Nurses’ Health Study).  No validated adjustments for sex or age on TyG or other markers in this context have been published. 

- **Confounding conditions/medications:**  Acute inflammatory states (infection, trauma) can raise CRP/TNFα independent of metabolic risk, confounding “inflammation” markers.  Corticosteroids or atypical antipsychotics can induce insulin resistance and hyperglycemia.  End-stage renal disease raises TG and C-reactive protein, complicating interpretation.  Metabolic biomarkers may be unreliable in pregnancy or acute illness. 

- **Evidence gaps:**  There is **no prospective validation of specific biomarker thresholds for cognitive outcomes**.  Ethnicity- or age-specific data are lacking.  Biomarker studies often do not account for APOE genotype or directly measure brain endpoints.  Causality is unproven: lowering one marker (e.g. TG with fibrates) has not been shown to reduce dementia risk specifically. 

- **Cautionary trial evidence:**  In ACCORD-MIND, aggressive glucose lowering in diabetics preserved brain volume but **did not improve cognitive scores**【90†L7-L10】.  Thus, improving the TyG index/glucose in diabetics may not translate to cognitive benefit.  Similarly, no trials have demonstrated that modifying these metabolic markers (beyond general risk factor control) reliably prevents dementia.  The platform must therefore communicate that this signal is prognostic and that modification of metabolic metrics has an *uncertain* effect on cognitive outcomes.
